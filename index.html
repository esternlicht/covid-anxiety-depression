When COVID-19 Cases Rise in the U.S., Anxiety and Depression Follow

The need to access mental health services across the country has never been greater.

By Emily Sternlicht

Dec. 16, 2021

A man holds his head in anxiety. Credit: Pixabay

The pandemic has taken a toll on Americans' mental health, and the recent discovery of the Omicron COVID-19 variant brings new concerns. A Gallup poll from November 2021 found that only 34% of U.S. adults say their mental health is "excellent" - a two decade low.

A CDC report from October 2021 examines U.S. Census Bureau Household Pulse Survey data from the beginning of the pandemic through June 2021, which tracked the the percentage of U.S. adults with symptoms of anxiety and depressive disorders.

After analyzing the data, the CDC found a sharp increase in anxiety and depression when COVID-19 cases were rising, and a decrease when COVID-19 cases started to fall. Even though these rates descreased, it was still higher overall as compared to 2019 levels.

“At the beginning of the pandemic there were substantial increaces in depression and anxiety in the U.S. and other locations that were hit hard,” said Professor Philip Yanos, director of the Clinical Psychology Training Progam at the John Jay College of Crimincal Justice.

Yanos said that anxiety varies from state to state. "In some states the worry might be about going back to in person things, or worry for infection," said Yanos, while other states might be worried about the loss of in-person activity due to COVID-19 related restrictions.

One recommendation is to be proactive about seeking mental health treatment, said Yanos. “The only thing we can do is help people cope," he said. "To be open to other ways of finding meaning in their lives that they might not be used to — that can give them comfort and solace.”

Nationwide, average anxiety increased 13% from August to December 2020 and then decreased 26.8% from December 2020 to June 2021.
